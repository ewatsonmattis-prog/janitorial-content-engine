Hi [First Name],

Most cold emails cleaning companies send get deleted in under three seconds.

Not because the company isn't good. Because the email looks like it was written for no one in particular — and facilities managers can spot a mail-merge pitch immediately.

Here's what actually happens on their end.

A facilities manager at a 12,000 sq ft office block gets 40-plus cold emails a week. Their job is to protect the building, keep costs controlled, and avoid complaints from staff. When your email lands and says something like "we offer professional commercial cleaning at competitive rates" — it tells them nothing about whether you understand their situation.

They move on.

What gets a response is specificity.

Facilities managers in offices, healthcare, and education are shortlisting contractors who sound like they've done this before — in their type of building, for their type of problem. That means referencing building size, cleaning frequency expectations, and sector-specific concerns. A healthcare FM worries about infection control. An education FM worries about term-time scheduling and safeguarding compliance. An office FM worries about out-of-hours access and presentation for clients.

A generic pitch addresses none of that.

The contractors winning these contracts are running a 3-email sequence — not a single blast. Email one opens a relevant, specific conversation. Email two adds a concrete proof point (a result, a case study, a relevant detail). Email three is a short, low-friction follow-up. That sequence, built around the recipient's sector and building type, converts at 3–5x the rate of a single generic pitch.

This isn't about writing longer emails. It's about writing the right email to the right person — and having a repeatable system to do it at scale.
