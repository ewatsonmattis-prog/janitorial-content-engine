**Preview Text:** It's not your list. It's what happens after the first message.

Hi [First Name],

Most cleaning companies sending cold emails make the same mistake. They send one message, get no reply, and assume the prospect isn't interested.

They move on. The opportunity doesn't.

Here's what's actually happening: office managers and facilities buyers receive a lot of supplier emails. Your first message rarely lands at the right moment. That doesn't mean the door is closed — it means your timing was off.

The companies winning office cleaning contracts consistently aren't the ones with the best subject lines. They're the ones with a follow-up system that stays visible without being annoying.

What that looks like in practice:

A first email that speaks directly to a problem the prospect has — staff turnover in their current cleaning team, audit failures, inconsistent results. Not a generic intro about your company.

A follow-up sequence of three to five touchpoints over two to four weeks. Short messages. Different angles. One ask per email.

A reason to reply that feels low-friction — not "let us quote you" but "would it be worth a ten-minute call to see if there's a fit?"

This isn't complicated. But most cleaning businesses don't do it because they're busy running operations, not running a sales pipeline.

The result? They rely on referrals and renewals, and their revenue stays unpredictable. When a contract ends, they're scrambling.

A cold email system built for office contract lead generation changes that. It creates a steady flow of conversations with decision-makers who already know your name before they get on a call with you.

You're not chasing. You're following up. There's a difference — and prospects feel it.
