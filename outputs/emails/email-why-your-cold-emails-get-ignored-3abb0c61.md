# Email Newsletter: Why your cold emails get ignored

**Subject Line:** Why your cold emails get ignored
**Preview Text:** Facilities managers delete generic pitches. Here's what they respond to instead.
**Word Count:** 312

---

Hi [First Name],

Most cold emails cleaning companies send get deleted in under three seconds.

Not because the service is bad. Because the email reads like it was written for anyone — which means it feels relevant to no one.

Facilities managers in offices, schools, and healthcare buildings receive outreach constantly. They're not looking for the cheapest quote or the longest list of services. They're looking for a contractor who clearly understands their environment.

That's the gap most cleaning companies miss.

When your email references their specific building type, the cleaning challenges that come with it, and the frequency that makes sense for their operation — it signals competence before you've even had a conversation. It shows you've done the work.

A 3-email sequence built around this principle — industry, building size, cleaning frequency — consistently converts at 3 to 5 times the rate of a generic pitch. The first email opens the door. The second adds credibility. The third asks for a specific next step.

This isn't complicated. But it does require you to stop sending the same email to every prospect and start treating each vertical as its own conversation.

A healthcare facility manager worries about infection control and compliance sign-off. An office building manager wants consistency and minimal disruption. A school facilities lead needs to work around term times and safeguarding requirements. These are different people with different problems — and your outreach should reflect that.

If your pipeline is quiet right now, the fix probably isn't more volume. It's more relevance.

Take 30 minutes to map out what each of your target verticals actually cares about. Then write three short emails — under 150 words each — that speak directly to those concerns. Send them on a 5-day cadence. Track replies, not open rates.

That's a cold email sequence worth running.
