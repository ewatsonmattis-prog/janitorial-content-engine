**Subject Line:** Why FMs ignore your cold emails
**Preview Text:** It's not your price. It's that your email could've been sent to anyone.

Hi [First Name],

Most cold emails to facilities managers get deleted in under three seconds. Not because the timing was wrong. Because the email read like it was written for no one in particular.

Facilities managers — in offices, healthcare, and education especially — are responsible for keeping complex buildings running. They get pitched constantly. Generic outreach like "we offer high-quality cleaning services at competitive rates" tells them nothing about whether you understand their environment.

Here's what actually works: a 3-email sequence built around specificity.

Email one references their industry and the cleaning challenges specific to it. A healthcare FM worries about infection control compliance. An education FM worries about turnaround time between terms. Lead with that, not your company history.

Email two mentions their building size or likely cleaning frequency — showing you've done basic research. Something like: "For a facility your size, most of our clients run a 5-day schedule with monthly deep cleans. Happy to share what that looks like if it's useful." That one line separates you from 90% of your competition.

Email three is a short, direct follow-up. No guilt. No "just circling back." Just a low-friction ask: a 10-minute call or a site visit offer. Keep it one sentence.

This sequence consistently converts at 3–5x the rate of a generic pitch — because it signals that you understand their building before they've said a word to you.

Facilities managers shortlist contractors who seem prepared. Your cold email is your first impression of how you'd operate on-site. Make it specific, make it relevant, and make it easy to say yes to.
